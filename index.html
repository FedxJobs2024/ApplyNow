Looking at a Familiar Place From a New Angle

A fly fisher discovers the wonders of a river’s surface

Ryan Chin

·

Follow

Published in

In Living Color

·
7 min read
·
Jan 24

I open the truck’s windows and inhale the sound of a rapid. If the trickle of a spring creek is a solo acoustic at a coffee shop, the Lower Deschutes River in Oregon is a stadium concert. With her glassy glides and rumbling rapids, she can be a head-banging metal show or a classical symphony.

The fading rapid gives way to the crunch of tires on snow and the chatter of a kingfisher. Slow is the name of the game; it’s Canyon time now. I’m not surprised to have the entire river and canyon to myself on this snowy December day. Breaking new tracks always fills the spirit; I am alone but not lonely.

Halo, my rescue pup from Texas, rams his snout out the window. He’s no stranger to this canyon. For over a year, he’s crashed through the sagebrush feeding the local ticks, scarfed cow patties, and to my worry — slurped riverside salmon so aged it ceased to be solid.

During the warm rattlesnake months, he’s not allowed to wander. If potential salmon poisoning is his greatest danger, so be it. Like crawling babies putting everything in their mouths, dogs eat a lot of shit, and I can’t stop him. The boundary between living freely and living safely is broad and subjective. Some would say my plans to swim solo in a 40-degree river is dangerous, but I’m determined to catch something besides fish on this trip.
